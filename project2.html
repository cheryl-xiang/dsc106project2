Cheryl Xiang — c3xiang@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Access to abortion clinics in the United States has declined in recent years.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Looking through the dataset, I realized that between 2014 and 2020, certain states had more extreme increases or decreases in percentages of counties without access to an abortion clinic. I also notice that there were metrics for both the difference in access between 2014 and 2020 as well as 2017 and 2020. The numbers of the first pair aligned better with my proposition, so I chose to focus on those data columns for this visualization. While this decision is not technically inaccurate since both timeframes are valid subsets of the original dataset, it could be considered mildly deceptive. By selectively presenting the timeframe that produced stronger declines, I can influence viewers' perceptions by focusing on the most persuasive subset of available data. Furthermore, I cherry picked the states where the percent of counties without access to a known abortion clinic was greater 2020 than 2014. To find these specific 10 states, I calculated the difference between the 2020 and 2014 columns as a new column and sorted by that column, isolating the top 10 decreases. Choosing only these top extremes is not generally a good representation of the entire country, but by specifying that I am only including the top 10 for "clarity" I aim to make this plot less deceptively obvious.
  • The original dataset provided numbers for the % of counties without known access to abortion clinics for 2014 and 2020. To improve readability and more intuitively convey trends, I reversed the metric to show the percentage of counties with access instead. This allowed me to design the visualization such that a decrease in bar height from 2014 to 2020 signals a negative outcome (loss of access). Additionally, by coloring the 2020 bars in red, a color conventionally associated with warning or decline, I reinforced the visual association between decreasing bar height, negative change, and undesirable outcomes. While these design choices do not alter or misrepresent the underlying data, they are intended to subtly influence viewers’ emotional perceptions. This approach could be considered to be mildly deceptive, particularly when compared to more neutral design strategies such as using a muted or neutral color palette.
  • Like my opposition graph, I considered using a simple bar chart with a single bar for each state showing just the percent change in counties with access to abortion clinics, but ultimately decided to use a grouped bar chart displaying both the 2014 and 2020 access percentages side by side for each state instead to visually emphasize a consistent decrease where viewers can immediately see that the bar for 2020 is consistently shorter than the bar for 2014. Although a single-bar chart summarizing percent change would have been simpler, it would also require more mental effort from viewers to interpret. The grouped bars make the decline in access more visually intuitive, aligning the message of the graph with viewers’ immediate visual interpretation. I also observed that even within the top 10 states, some experienced relatively modest declines in access, with one state showing only an 8% change. A single bar chart would have drawn attention to the small magnitude of these changes, so I opted for a grouped bar chart to divert viewers' attention to the existence of a decrease rather than the specific size of each decline.
  • Based on these design choices, I would give this visualization a deception score of deception score of -1. While my visualization choices did not directly alter raw data, they did selectively emphasize the strongest declines in access and use color and shape to influence emotional interpretation. Overall, I would consider this plot to be somewhat deceptive

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Similarly to the previous visualization, certain columnns from 2017 and 2020 were more strongly in support of the opposing claim, so I utilized those for this visualization. As stated previously, this could be considered mildly deceptive, as it somewhat obscures a subset of the raw data provided. The specific metric I used for this plot was the % change in the number of abortion clinics between 2017 and 2020 for each U.S. State. In order to find the data points that would be most convincing for arguing against my proposition statement, I sorted this column in descending order to find the 10 states with the largest positive changes which would also be the 10 states with the largest increase in number of abortion clinics from 2017 to 2020. This is another instance of cherry picking data. Unlike in the previous visualization, I did not clarify in the graph itself that only the top 10 states were being shown. As a result, this visualization is arguably more deceptive than the proposition side graph, as it may give viewers the impression that such increases were representative of national trends, rather than being exceptional cases.
  • To further the impression that the depicted increases in abortion clinic numbers were broadly representative of national trends, I mapped each state to its U.S. Census region and included the region name alongside each state on the x-axis. The top 10 states included states from the South, Midwest, and West. This design choice subtly encourages viewers to interpret the improvements as regionally widespread rather than isolated to a few outliers. Even though only 10 states were shown, the fact that they spanned multiple regions visually reinforced the idea of a nationwide trend. Additionally, the presence of several states from the South and Midwest —regions typically perceived as more politically conservative— helped make the visualization more convincing by highlighting gains in areas where viewers' might expect a decline in access to abortion, making the depicted trend feel more significant and credible. Furthermore, I chose the title "Gains in Abortion Clinic Access Across Regions (2017–2020)," with a smaller subtitle clarifying that the South, Midwest, and West were represented while the Northeast was not. This title was used to frame my visualization as a story of regional leadership in positive change while acknowledging the absence of any states from the Northeast. The Northeast is traditionally considered to be more politically liberal and already maintained relatively high levels of access prior to 2017, so its absence from the largest increases should not particularly surprising to viewers, so a simple acknowledgement of this fact can help dissuade any suspicion regarding the credibility of this visualization.
  • For the opposition visualization, I decided that a simple bar chart displaying the percent change in the number of clinics for each state would be an effective design choice. Unlike in the proposition side where I wanteded to emphasize a directional decrease (by using a grouped bar chart), here my focus was on highlighting positive improvements without creating extra visual complexity. I chose a blue color palette for this visualization because it is often perceived as a neutral or calming color. This helped reinforce the message that the increases in clinic access were optimistic developments. Within the palette, I used a gradient based on percent increase and assigned darker shades of blue to states with larger gains to highlight them without using additional annotations or disrupting the overall harmony of the design by adding visual clutter. Using lighter shades of blue for states with less drastic increases also helps de-emphasize smaller improvements while naturally drawing the viewer’s attention toward states with larger gains.

While my visualization choices did not directly alter raw data, they did focus on the top 10 states with the largest increases, without clearly indicating that limitation in the graph itself. I also used colors and the addition of regions to create a stronger optimistic viewer perspective. Overall, I would consider this plot to be deceptive

Final Reflection

Throughout this process, I learned a lot about how subtle design choices can significantly influence how the data you present in interpreted. While I have come across plenty of data visualizations in my everyday life, I don't often stop to consider how I could be being subconciously infleunced by the creator's visualization choices. I was surprised by how much changed small aspects of a graph such as colors or titles/subtitles could change the story presented by the underlying data. Some aspects of the project were more straightforward, such as choosing chart types and color palettes, while others proved to be more difficult (deciding on chart titles, finding the most suitable data points, making any deceptive tactics used less obvious) I also had access to multiple timeframes and metrics for both the proposition and opposition views, which meant that simply choosing what data to highlight already involved a degree of subjectivity.

Regarding “ethical analysis and visualization”, I would define this concept to more of a spectrum rather than a binary definition of "ethical" or "inethical." A visualization can be factually accurate and still be misleading if it emphasizes certain patterns, omits context, or guides emotion through color and layout. I also believe there exists a level of subjectivity when it comes to deciding whether these design choices are considered "ethical analysis." There are also levels of deception within these visual choices. For instance, showing only the top 10 states with the biggest increases is not necessarily inethical if clearly stated, but omitting that clarification makes the visualization more deceptively persuasive. In some cases, even raw data itself can be misleading to some viewers. In the case of this particular project, declines and increases to abortion clinic access were given in percentages without any information on specific numbers. This means that if a state changed from no abortion clinics to one abortion clinic, it would be considered a 100% increase in access. Without context on the absolute number of clinics, viewers might overestimate the significance of certain changes, even if the percentage growth seems dramatic. Another limitation of the dataset is that it defines access at the state level, without adjusting for important contextual factors like state size or population density. This means that the data does not necessarily reflect meaningful improvements or declines in access for individual residents. For example, a state opening a new clinic might dramatically improve access in a small, densely populated state, but make very little difference in a large, rural state where travel distances remain high. From this project, I have come to realize how important transparency withinin your visualizations is when it comes to maintaining ethical analysis and visualization practices.

I would draw the line between “acceptable” persuasion and “misleading” design based on viewer awareness and data integrity. If the viewer is reasonably informed about the scope and framing of the data, and the visualization does not distort the underlying data itself, I would consider the design ethically acceptable for most cases. But when design choices intentionally or unintentionally obscure key limitations (like only showing outliers or disregarding missing data) without clear context, the visualization crosses the line into being misleading. Small decisions such as claiming that states were ommitted for "clarity" rather than because they did not support the proposition can be considered to be somewhat misleading. This project has shown me that persuasive visualization is not inherently unethical, but it requires intentional reflection and respect for the viewer’s ability to interpret honestly.
